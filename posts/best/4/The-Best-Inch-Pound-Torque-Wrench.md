### What is an inch pound torque wrench?

An inch pound torque wrench is a precision tool used to apply specific torque (rotational force) to bolts and nuts. The "inch pound" measurement indicates the unit of torque, with one inch pound equal to the force of one pound applied one inch from the pivot point. 

These wrenches feature a gauge that indicates when the desired torque has been reached, allowing for consistent tightening without over-tightening, which can damage threads or strip bolts. 


### How do you read an inch pound torque wrench?

The wrench displays torque readings on a calibrated scale. Some models have a simple click mechanism that stops when the desired torque is reached, while others use a gauge that shows the applied torque value in inch pounds. 

For click models, you adjust the torque setting by turning a dial or knurled ring. The wrench 'clicks' when the preset torque is achieved, indicating proper tightening.

### What's the difference between inch pounds and foot pounds?

Inch pounds and foot pounds are both units of torque, but they represent different distances. One foot pound is equal to 12 inch pounds (12 inches is one foot). 

When measuring torque, foot pounds are often used for larger tightening applications due to the longer measurement, while inch pounds are common for smaller, more precise work. 


### What kind of torque wrench should I buy?

Choose a torque wrench that matches your specific needs in terms of torque range, weight, and style (click or gauge). Common ranges for home and automotive use are 10 to 100 inch pounds for click-type and 30 to 200 inch pounds for gauge models. 

Consider the durability requirements of your application - if you're working in harsh environments, look for a wrench with a protective coating or construction suitable for those conditions.

Yes, torque wrenches need regular calibration to maintain their accuracy. The calibration process involves checking or adjusting the wrench's mechanism to ensure it meets manufacturer specifications for torque application. 

Professional tools are typically sent to certified calibration centers, while home users might use a torque reference standard to verify their wrench's accuracy.
