When shopping for a distortion pedal, you will come across two types of bypass systems: true bypass and buffered bypass. Buffered bypasspedals tend to have a warmer tone, while true-bypass pedals offer a cleaner, more accurate sound.

### Exactly how does a distortion pedal operate?

A distortion pedal functions by clipping the peaks of the input signal, causing them to become more rounded and compressed. The clipping is achieved through the use of diodes or transistors which act as nonlinear elements in the circuit. The nonlinearity results in the peaks becoming cut off, while the valleys remain untouched. This results in the clipping effect. As the clipping level increases, the sound becomes more distorted, and additional harmonics are added to the original signal.

There are various types of distortion circuits, such as silicon and germanium diode distortion, IC-based, and tube-driven distortion. These circuits produce varying levels of harmonic content, resulting in different distortion characteristics. The tone control on a distortion pedal helps adjust the frequency balance of the distorted signal, allowing the user to fine-tune the tone of their sound.
