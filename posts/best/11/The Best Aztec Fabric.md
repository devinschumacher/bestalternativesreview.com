## FAQ


### What is Aztec fabric?

Aztec fabric is a traditional textile pattern inspired by ancient Aztec designs. It features prominent diagonal lines forming diamond shapes, often in vibrant colors. 

The pattern typically includes geometric shapes and motifs, combining red, black, and white with other bold colors in modern interpretations. 


### What are the main characteristics of Aztec fabric?

Aztec fabric combines durability with traditional patterns. It's often made from cotton or cotton blends, offering both softness and longevity. 

The texture typically ranges from medium to slightly thick, with patterns that vary from simple to complex designs. 


### How should Aztec fabric be maintained?

Care instructions vary by material, but most Aztec fabrics require washing with mild detergent and cold water. Avoid bleach and harsh chemicals. 

Most designs can be machine washed, though air drying is recommended to maintain the pattern's integrity. Ironing is usually necessary for best results. 


### What are the common usages of Aztec fabric?

Aztec fabric is popular for upholstery, creating bold indoor accents through curtains, pillows, and upholstery. Its durability makes it suitable for commercial applications. 

The pattern also works well in textiles for bedding, from duvet covers to decorative throws, and can be found in various accessories like handbags and scarves. 


### What colors are typically used in Aztec patterns?

The traditional Aztec color scheme includes black, white, and red, though modern interpretations can incorporate a wide range of colors. 

The diamond patterns often feature black outlining with red and white filling, creating a distinctive repeating design across the fabric. 


### How should Aztec patterns be scaled for different applications?

Aztec patterns look best when scaled appropriately for their use. Smaller patterns work well in bedding and accessories, while larger designs suit upholstery and walls. 

The pattern density should match the space's scale - smaller rooms look best with less dense patterns, while larger rooms can handle more intricate designs.
