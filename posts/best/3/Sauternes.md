### Historical Context

Sauternes has been a popular dessert wine since the 16th century. Over the centuries, it has earned a reputation for its exquisite flavors and balance, with the "noble rot" fungus playing a vital role in its production. Known as "noble" because of its positive effect on the wine, it actually develops on overripe grapes, causing them to shrink and lose water while preserving their sugars. This creates a unique environment for the development of aromatic compounds that give Sauternes its signature sweetness while retaining some acidity for balance.

Sauternes can be categorized into various styles based on the proportion of white wine varieties, sugar levels, and aging process. There are three main styles: Classical, Sweet White, and Dry White. Once touched by the noble rot, the grapes are harvested and transported to the winery, where they are pressed to extract the juice. This juice is then fermented, often with the addition of yeast, to transform the sugars in the grapes into alcohol. Subsequently, the wine undergoes a period of aging in oak barrels for several years, during which time its flavor and aroma profiles develop and mature completely.
