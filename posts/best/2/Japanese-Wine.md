### What is Japanese wine?

Japanese wine, also known as Nihonshu, is a uniquely crafted beverage made from fermented rice that does not contain any added grape juice or other grape-derived products. This differentiates it from Western wines, which are mainly produced from grape fermentation. It's also often slightly sweeter and has a distinctive flavor profile, making it stand out among other wine varieties. 

The history of Japanese wine dates back thousands of years. According to historical records, sake – also brewed from fermented rice – was already being consumed in the Orient during the time of the Chinese Zhou dynasty (1046-256 BCE). Over time, the production techniques and flavors of rice-based beverages evolved. 


### What are the different types of Japanese wine?

Two well-known types of Japanese wine include: Nihonshu and Sake. Both are rice-based fermented beverages but have some differences in their production process, flavor profiles, and intended uses. For instance, Nihonshu is usually slightly sweeter and has a lower alcohol content, making it more suitable for drinking during meals, while Sake is generally stronger and intended for drinking during ceremonies or special occasions. 

Additionally, Japanese wine often includes terms like Junmai, Junmai Ginjo, and Junmai Daiginjo. These terms refer to the level of polish of the rice (known as the polishing rate) and the class of the wine. The more polished the rice is, the lighter the wine will be and the more it will express the pure flavor of the rice. Conversely, wines made from less polished rice will have more robust flavors.

### How is Japanese wine produced?

Japanese wine production is a precise and artisanal process, much like making French champagne. The process starts with the selection of high-quality glutinous rice. The rice grains are then polished to remove the outer layer, which contains the majority of the protein. The resulting polished rice is then washed, steamed, and cooled down to halt the baking process. Yeast culture is added to start the fermentation process, which results in the formation of alcohol. 

The fermentation process in Japanese wine takes anywhere from a few days to several weeks, depending on factors such as yeast strain, temperature, and humidity. After fermentation, the sake is left to age, which can last several months or up to a few years for the highest grade of sake. During the ageing process, additional flavors develop, and some impurities break down, resulting in a smoother sake that has developed its characteristic aroma and taste.

### How should I pour and serve Japanese wine?

Serving Japanese wine is an important part of fully appreciating its many nuances. To pour, you should tilt the bottle and pour slowly using a steady hand. The glass should be filled about an inch or so from the top, leaving some headspace for the foam that may form with more premium Sakes. Once poured, give the glass a gentle swirl to promote proper aeration.
