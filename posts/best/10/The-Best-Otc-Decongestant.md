## FAQ

### What is an OTC decongestant?

Over-the-counter (OTC) decongestants are medications available without a prescription that help reduce nasal congestion. Common ingredients include pseudoephedrine, phenylephrine, and oxymetazoline, each working in different ways to relieve stuffy noses. 

These products typically come in various forms such as tablets, capsules, liquids, and nasal sprays, offering different durations of relief and suitable applications based on the severity of congestion. 


### How do they work?

OTC decongestants work by constricting blood vessels in your nasal passages, reducing swelling and opening the airways. This action helps relieve symptoms like runny nose and congestion caused by colds, flu, or allergies. 

The mechanism varies between oral and topical forms: oral medications affect the central nervous system, while topical sprays directly constrict nasal blood vessels. 

![Otc-Decongestant-5](https://imagedelivery.net/vy2bglCGN6hEeWOnSe2c7A/Otc-Decongestant-5/h=540,fit=pad,background=black)


### What are the key ingredients?

Pseudoephedrine and phenylephrine are the most common active ingredients in OTC decongestants. Both work similarly to reduce nasal congestion, though pseudoephedrine tends to have fewer side effects. 

Less common ingredients include oxymetazoline, phenylpropanolamine (now less common due to safety concerns), and ephedrine derivatives (usually not available in the US). 


### How long do they take to work?

Oral decongestants typically begin to work within 30-60 minutes after taking the medication. The full effect usually lasts 4-6 hours. 

Nasal sprays usually start working within 5-10 minutes and provide relief for about 4-6 hours. Continuous use of sprays should not exceed 3 days to avoid rebound congestion. 

![Otc-Decongestant-6](https://imagedelivery.net/vy2bglCGN6hEeWOnSe2c7A/Otc-Decongestant-6/h=540,fit=pad,background=black)


### What are the side effects?

The most common side effects include restlessness, insomnia, elevated blood pressure, and dizziness. These effects are more pronounced with oral medications than nasal sprays. 

Overuse can lead to dependence and rebound congestion. If you experience severe side effects or your symptoms persist after 7-10 days, consult a healthcare provider. 


### Who should avoid them?

Individuals with uncontrolled hypertension, heart disease, thyroid disorders, or enlarged prostate should avoid these medications without medical consultation. 

Children under 2 years old and pregnant or breastfeeding women should only use decongestants under the guidance of a healthcare provider, as safety data for these populations is limited.
