## FAQ

![Radon-Test-4](https://imagedelivery.net/vy2bglCGN6hEeWOnSe2c7A/Radon-Test-4/h=540,fit=pad,background=black)


### What is radon, and why should I test for it?

Radon is a naturally occurring, odorless, colorless gas produced by the decay of uranium in soil, rock, and water. It's a known carcinogen that can accumulate in buildings and increase the risk of lung cancer, particularly in smokers and those exposed to secondhand smoke. 

Testing for radon is important because it's the second leading cause of lung cancer in the United States, after smoking. Even if you don't smoke, exposure to high levels of radon can pose significant health risks. Testing helps determine whether mitigation measures are necessary to protect you and your family's health. 


### When should I test for radon?

The recommended time to test for radon is during the coldest months of the year, typically from November to March. This period, when doors and windows are more likely to be closed, provides the most accurate representation of year-round radon levels. 

However, you can test at any time of year. If you've recently renovated your home or suspect high radon levels for other reasons, testing earlier can give you valuable information about your living environment's radon concentrations. 

![Radon-Test-5](https://imagedelivery.net/vy2bglCGN6hEeWOnSe2c7A/Radon-Test-5/h=540,fit=pad,background=black)


### What are the most accurate radon testing methods?

The most accurate radon testing methods are continuous monitors, which measure radon levels in real-time and provide detailed concentration data over time. These devices offer the highest accuracy but require periodic calibration and battery replacement. 

Alpha track detectors are another accurate option, using special film to measure radon damage over 90 days. While less expensive than continuous monitors, they offer reliable results and are easier to install and maintain. 


### How do I interpret radon test results?

Radon test results are typically measured in picocuries per liter (pCi/L). The Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) recommends taking action to reduce radon levels if your test result is 4 pCi/L or higher. Levels between 2 and 4 pCi/L indicate moderate risk, and anything below 2 pCi/L is considered safe. 

Remember that radon concentrations can vary significantly from room to room and even within the same room. If you receive an unexpected high result, consider testing multiple areas of your home for more accurate data.

### Where can I purchase a radon test kit?

Radon test kits are widely available from home improvement stores, online retailers, and some hardware stores. Many states also have designated radon testing supply locations or organizations that offer kits. 

When purchasing, look for EPA-approved kits that can provide reliable, accurate results. The cost typically ranges from $20 to $100, depending on the type and brand of the kit. 


### What should I do if my test results are high?

If your radon test results are 4 pCi/L or higher, you should consider professional radon mitigation services. These services use specialized equipment and techniques to reduce indoor radon levels effectively. 

Professional mitigation typically includes installing a vent system that safely removes radon from beneath the building's foundation. While costly (usually between $800 to $2,500), it's a proven method to significantly reduce radon levels and improve indoor air quality.
