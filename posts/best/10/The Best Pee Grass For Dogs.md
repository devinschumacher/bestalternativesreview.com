## FAQ


### What is pee grass?

Pee grass, also known as urine grass or bladder grass, is a specific type of grass that appears in dog urine spots. Dogs have a natural instinct to mark their territory, and when they urinate on certain grasses, it causes changes in the plant's appearance over time. 

Overexposure to dog urine can cause yellowing, browning, or stunted growth in these grasses, making them easily recognizable as pee grass spots in a yard. 


### How can I identify pee grass spots?

Look for areas in your lawn where the grass appears discolored, yellowed, or stunted. These spots typically form near where your dog has urinated, often in small, circular patterns. 

The grass within these spots may show visible changes even after you've addressed the underlying issue, helping you locate re-urination hotspots. 


### What causes pee grass?

Pee grass results from the ammonium in dog urine damaging nearby plants. While healthy grass can generally recover from occasional exposure, repeated urination in the same spot causes chronic damage. 

The specific composition of a dog's urine, including pH levels and microbial content, can vary between individuals and contribute to the development of pee grass spots. 


### Is pee grass harmful to my lawn?

Pee grass damage affects only the grass in the immediate area of concentrated urine, leaving the rest of your lawn unaffected. The damaged patches typically recover when the urinary issue is resolved. 

If your dog has a health condition that increases urine volume or acidity, this could lead to more widespread lawn damage beyond typical pee grass spots. 


### How can I prevent pee grass?

Addressing canine health issues that increase urine volume or acidity is key to preventing pee grass. Regular veterinary check-ups and appropriate diet can help maintain healthy urinary function. 

Ensure your dog has access to plenty of clean water, which can dilute urine and reduce bladder irritants. Proper potty training and regular breaks during long walks can also prevent excessive urination in the same spot. 


### What can I do about existing pee grass spots?

Continue to monitor your dog's urination habits and address any underlying health issues. Regularly rotate where your dog urinates to prevent repeated damage in the same spots. 

If existing pee grass spots persist despite behavioral changes, treating them as you would any other lawn problem – through proper mowing, fertilizing, and irrigation – can help promote new grass growth.
