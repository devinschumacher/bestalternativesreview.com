## FAQ


### What exactly are Fireballs?

Fireballs are sugar-free candy balls that resemble jujubes but contain a uniquely shaped firework-like center. When bitten, the candy shell cracks to reveal a brightly colored, sparkling center that appears to "fire" or sparkle. 

Each candy comes in a variety of flavors including Traditional, Blackberry, Green Apple, Watermelon, Raspberry, and Strawberry Lemonade. 


### What makes Fireballs different from regular candy?

The key difference is their interactive "firework" center. When you bite through the candy exterior, you're rewarded with a display of color and sparkle, creating a sensory experience beyond just eating candy. 

Unlike traditional candy, which melts in your mouth, Fireballs deliver a distinct popping sensation as you break through the layers. 


### How do you eat Fireballs?

Using your teeth, carefully crack the candy shell to reveal the firework center. The interior may be hot upon first exposure, so wait a moment for it to cool before further consumption. 

Fireballs can be enjoyed as a standalone treat or used as conversation starters during gatherings, thanks to their unusual properties. 


### Are Fireballs safe for kids?

Yes, Fireballs are safe for children to enjoy. They contain no actual fire or hot elements but caution is advised to prevent choking, especially for very young children. 

Parents should supervise children while they're eating Fireballs to ensure proper handling of the candy. 


### How long do Fireballs last?

Once opened, Fireballs should be consumed within 1-2 weeks for best quality. They can be stored in an airtight container at room temperature to maintain their sparkle. 

Since they're sugar-free, preservation is not an issue, but the candy's color and sparkle may fade over time if exposed to air. 


### What's the history behind Fireballs?

Fireballs were introduced in 1999 by the Swedish confectionery company Bonitz. They quickly gained popularity for their unique candy design. 

The candies have since become a Christmas tradition in many households, though their limited time availability in Sweden has led to their occasional scarcity.
