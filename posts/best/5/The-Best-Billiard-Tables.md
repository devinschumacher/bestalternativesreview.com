## FAQ


### What are the standard sizes for billiard tables?

Billiard tables come in several standard sizes, with the most common being 7.5 feet, 8 feet, and 9 feet in length. The standard playing surface measure 3.5 feet to 4 feet in width. The 8-foot table is the most popular size for home use, offering a good balance of play space and room for movement. 

Smaller 7.5-foot tables are suitable for very limited spaces, while 9-foot tables provide more playing surface at the expense of mobility. Professional competition tables are typically 10 feet in length. 


### How much space do I need for a billiard table?

At minimum, measure twice the length of the table plus 36 inches (1 foot) for clearance on all sides. This allows for both playing space and movement around the table. For example, an 8-foot table requires 18 feet of available space (8 feet x 2 + 36 inches). 

Also consider overhead clearance, as billiard balls can hit ceiling fans or lights. A clearance height of 7 feet 8 inches from the playing surface to the ceiling is typically recommended. 


### What materials are billiard tables made from?

The primary material for the playing surface is slate, with 3/4-inch thick slabs being standard. The table frame is usually constructed from wood (often maple or mahogany) or steel. The legs can be wood, metal, or engineered composite materials. 

Modern tables may incorporate various materials for the playing surface covering, including cloth (commonly green with white stars), resin, or rubber. These covering materials vary in durability and play characteristics. 


### How do I choose the right cushion material?

The most common cushion materials are leather and rubber. Leather cushions provide a traditional feel and maintain their shape well, while rubber cushions offer more consistent bounce and are easier to maintain. 

For home use, leather is generally preferred for its aesthetic appeal and durability. Professional tables often use rubber cushions for their superior performance characteristics in competitive play. 


### What's the difference between a drop leaf and a cabinet table?

A drop leaf table features one or both end panels that can be lowered to store the additional playing surface. When not in use, these panels fold down to save space. Drop leaf tables are popular for limited spaces but may lack stability when fully extended. 

A cabinet table stores its extra playing surface under the main playing area, typically accessed through doors that slide or swing open. These tables are more stable and easier to maintain, but require additional storage space. 


### Do I need a specific skill level to play on different table sizes?

Smaller tables (7.5 feet and 8 feet) are generally easier for beginners to manage, as the ball runs slower and takes more time to react. The larger 9-foot tables challenge players with longer shots and more complex positioning. 

Advanced players may prefer the 9-foot table for its increased playing space and the strategic challenges it presents. The 8-foot table provides a versatile option suitable for players of all skill levels.
