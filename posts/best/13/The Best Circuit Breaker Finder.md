## FAQ


### What is a circuit breaker?

A circuit breaker is an electrical switch designed to protect an electrical circuit from damage caused by excess current from an overload or short circuit. It functions by interrupting electrical flow when current exceeds a safe limit, which helps prevent fires and other electrical hazards. 

Circuit breakers are rated based on amperage capacity and can be reset to restore power once the fault causing the overload has been corrected, unlike a fuse which must be replaced after a blown event. 


### How do I choose the right circuit breaker?

The appropriate circuit breaker depends on the amperage requirements of the electrical devices connected to it. Residential breakers typically range from 15 to 200 amps, with common sizes being 15, 20, 30, and 40 amps. The total load of all connected devices must be considered to prevent overloading the circuit. 

Additional considerations include voltage rating (120V or 240V), interrupting rating (the maximum current it can safely interrupt), and the number of poles (single-pole for 120V or double-pole for 240V systems). 


### What are the different types of circuit breakers?

There are several types of circuit breakers, including: - Molded Case Circuit Breakers (MCCB): Common in residential and light commercial applications, these typically range up to 600A. - Air Circuit Breakers: Used in industrial and commercial settings, these can handle higher currents and are typically hermetically sealed. 

Smart Circuit Breakers: Featuring advanced monitoring and communication capabilities, these devices can provide real-time data on circuit performance and detect changes that may indicate a fault. 


### How do circuit breakers work?

Circuit breakers contain a piece of metal that heats up when excessive current flows through the circuit. When the temperature reaches a preset point, a bimetallic strip or thermal magnet causes the breaker to interrupt the electrical connection, effectively shutting off power to the circuit. 

Magnetic Breakers: These use a coil that becomes magnetized when overcurrent flows, causing a movable piece to lift and break the circuit. Electronic Breakers: Modern devices use circuitry to measure current flow and activate a relay when levels exceed the rated capacity. 


### How often should circuit breakers be inspected or replaced?

It's recommended to inspect circuit breakers during routine home maintenance, checking for signs of wear, damage, or loose connections. Breakers typically last between 10 to 20 years, depending on usage and environmental conditions. Signs that may indicate replacement include frequent tripping (not due to overload), visible damage, or difficulty resetting the breaker. 

Regular inspection of the breaker panel, including the main breaker and individual circuit breakers, can help identify potential issues before they lead to electrical problems. Professional inspection every 6-12 years is advisable, especially if the panel is older than 25 years. 


### What should I do if a circuit breaker trips frequently?

A circuit breaker that trips frequently may indicate an overload, a short circuit, or a ground fault. Check the connected devices for excessive power usage or damaged wiring. Loose connections, poor installation, or outdated wiring can also cause the breaker to trip. 

If you determine that the overload is due to the actual usage of your devices, consider upgrading to a breaker with a higher amperage rating. For recurring trips, consult with a licensed electrician to identify and address the underlying issue.
