## FAQ


### What makes a knife suitable for sashimi?

A sashimi knife should have a blade that's at least 8 inches long and made from high-carbon stainless steel for durability and sharpness. The blade needs to be incredibly thin and razor-sharp to slice fish without tearing it. 

The handle should provide excellent grip and balance, with a traditional Japanese ray-ray or sharkskin grip that prevents slippage during precise cuts. 


### How should I maintain a sashimi knife?

After each use, clean the blade with mild soap and water, then dry it thoroughly. The blade should be sharpened professionally every 3-6 months, or whenever you notice it's not slicing cleanly. 

Store the knife in its dedicated sheath or block to prevent damage and rust. The blade should not touch other metals to prevent oxidation. 


### What's the difference between a Western and Japanese sashimi knife?

Western sashimi knives have straighter blades with a slight curve, while Japanese versions are more curved and have a distinctive double-bevel edge. The Japanese design allows for more precise, flick-cut techniques. 

The Japanese blade is typically thinner and lighter, optimized for slicing through delicate fish without crushing the flesh. Western knives often have a heavier blade for cutting through tougher meats. 


### How can I tell if a sashimi knife is sharp enough?

Visual inspection alone isn't sufficient. Hold the blade against a piece of paper - if you can't feel the paper tearing between your fingers, it needs sharpening. You should also be able to cut through firm ingredients like cucumber slices without crushing them. 

The blade should maintain its sharpness for multiple cuts without needing to be repositioned. If you find yourself constantly adjusting your grip or angle, the blade isn't holding its edge properly. 


### Can I use a regular kitchen knife for sashimi?

While it's possible to use a regular kitchen knife, it's not ideal. The blade will likely be too thick and heavy to slice through fish effectively. You'll also have a higher risk of damaging the delicate flesh. 

Using a proper sashimi knife ensures clean cuts, prevents fish from tearing, and makes the slicing process more efficient and controlled. 


### How often should I replace my sashimi knife?

With regular use, a high-quality sashimi knife should last 5-10 years with proper maintenance. Signs you need a new blade include dulling after sharpening, visible damage to the edge, or difficulty maintaining a clean cut. 

Consider replacing your knife when you notice it's no longer slicing through ingredients as cleanly as when you first purchased it. The blade might also show signs of wear, such as visible nicks or uneven edge retention.
